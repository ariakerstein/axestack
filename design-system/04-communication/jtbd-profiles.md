# JTBD Profiles

**Before building any UI, identify which profile sees it. Design for THEIR emotional state, not a generic user.**

---

## 1. Newly Diagnosed (Week 0-4)

### Jobs
- **Functional**: Understand what I have. Know what tests I need. Find the right doctor. Know if my treatment plan is complete.
- **Emotional**: Want to feel IN CONTROL. Want to feel HOPEFUL. Don't want to feel HELPLESS. Don't want to feel STUPID for not understanding medical terms.
- **Social**: Want to be seen as PROACTIVE and STRONG. Don't want to be seen as a VICTIM or BURDEN.

### Profile
- **Goals**: Get a clear plan. Understand the basics. Not miss anything critical.
- **Aspirations**: "I want to look back and know I did everything right."
- **Fears**: Missing a test. Getting the wrong treatment. Being too late. Not understanding what doctors are saying.
- **What keeps them up**: "Am I going to die?" "Is my doctor good enough?" "What if we're missing something?"

### History
- **What triggered change**: Diagnosis. Doctor said words they didn't understand. Googled and got overwhelmed. Friend/family said "get a second opinion."
- **What they tried**: Google (terrifying). Patient forums (overwhelming). Called a second hospital (weeks-long wait, $4000).
- **What worked**: When someone gave them a CLEAR, SHORT list of things to do.
- **What failed**: Information dumps. Generic advice. Being told to "just trust your doctor."

### Key Objections
1. "I already have a doctor" → Frame as SUPPORTING their doctor, not replacing
2. "I'm too overwhelmed to deal with this" → "Just one step" — we do the work
3. "How is AI qualified?" → Board-certified oncologists review everything
4. "I can't afford this" → Free checklist, premium is $199 vs $4,000

### AHA Moment
"Here's your checklist — 3 tests your plan may be missing." Specific, actionable, personal.

### Design Implications
- SINGLE next step. No complex navigation.
- Large, clear CTA. "Get Your Checklist" not "Explore Our Platform."
- Minimal text. Front-load the benefit.
- Trust signals visible immediately (badges, credentials).
- Progressive disclosure for medical details.

## 2. Treatment-Stage (Months 3-18)

### Jobs
- **Functional**: Manage side effects. Track appointments. Know if treatment is working. Find better options if it's not.
- **Emotional**: Want to feel PREPARED for each appointment. Want to feel INFORMED. Don't want to feel BLINDSIDED by test results. Don't want to feel PASSIVE in their care.
- **Social**: Want to be seen as HANDLING IT. Don't want to be seen as FALLING APART.

### Profile
- **Goals**: Stay on top of treatment. Manage the day-to-day. Know the right questions to ask.
- **Aspirations**: "I want to walk into every appointment prepared."
- **Fears**: Surprise bad news. Side effects getting worse. Missing an appointment or test. Being a burden on family.
- **What keeps them up**: "Is the treatment working?" "What are these new symptoms?" "What questions should I ask tomorrow?"

### History
- **Triggered by**: A confusing appointment. A test result they didn't understand. Feeling lost between appointments.
- **What they tried**: Taking notes in appointments (hard when scared). Asking friends (they don't know). Patient portals (confusing).
- **What worked**: Specific questions to ask their doctor. Understanding what their numbers mean.
- **What failed**: Generic cancer information. Advice from people with different cancer types.

### Key Objections
1. "I'm already in treatment, it's too late" → "It's never too late to optimize your care"
2. "My doctor has it handled" → "We help you ask better questions, not replace your doctor"
3. "I don't have energy for this" → "5 minutes before your appointment"

### AHA Moment
"Here are 5 questions to ask at tomorrow's appointment — specific to YOUR cancer type and stage."

### Design Implications
- Appointment-centric UX. "Your next appointment" as primary frame.
- Question generation front and center.
- Timeline/progress view of treatment.
- Quick actions: "Prep for appointment" / "What does this result mean?"

## 3. Recurrent/Advanced (Year 1+)

### Jobs
- **Functional**: Find new options. Get access to clinical trials. Get opinions from specialists. Explore everything.
- **Emotional**: Want to feel THERE ARE STILL OPTIONS. Want to feel EMPOWERED. Don't want to feel ABANDONED by the system. Don't want to feel HOPELESS.
- **Social**: Want to be seen as STILL FIGHTING. Don't want to be pitied.

### Profile
- **Goals**: Exhaust every option. Find cutting-edge treatments. Connect with top specialists.
- **Aspirations**: "I want to know I tried everything."
- **Fears**: Running out of options. Being told "there's nothing more we can do." Missing a trial they qualified for.
- **What keeps them up**: "Is there a trial I don't know about?" "Should I go to a different hospital?" "Am I getting the most aggressive treatment possible?"

### History
- **Triggered by**: Recurrence. Treatment stopped working. Doctor mentioned "palliative" for the first time.
- **What they tried**: Multiple hospitals. Online trial searches (overwhelming). Support groups. Alternative medicine.
- **What worked**: Finding a specialist who knew about new options. Getting into a clinical trial.
- **What failed**: Being told to "stay positive." Generic trial searches with hundreds of irrelevant results.

### Key Objections
1. "I've already tried everything" → Show options they haven't seen (trial matching by biomarker)
2. "Trials are a last resort" → "5% enroll, 95% miss potential options"
3. "I don't want to be a guinea pig" → Reframe as accessing tomorrow's medicine today

### AHA Moment
"3 clinical trials within 50 miles match your specific biomarkers — and you're eligible."

### Design Implications
- Clinical trial matching as PRIMARY feature.
- Biomarker-aware recommendations.
- Research-forward: latest studies, emerging treatments.
- Power-user features: detailed filtering, comparison tools.

## 4. Caregiver

### Jobs
- **Functional**: Coordinate care without repeating information. Keep family updated. Track appointments and medications. Research on behalf of patient.
- **Emotional**: Want to feel USEFUL and IN CONTROL of what they can control. Don't want to feel HELPLESS watching someone they love suffer. Don't want to feel like they're FAILING.
- **Social**: Want to be seen as a GOOD caregiver. Don't want to be seen as OVERBEARING or CONTROLLING.

### Profile
- **Goals**: Be the best advocate possible. Not drop any balls. Keep the patient's life as normal as possible.
- **Aspirations**: "I want them to focus on healing while I handle everything else."
- **Fears**: Missing something important. Making a wrong decision. Burning out. Not being there enough.
- **What keeps them up**: "Did I ask the right questions?" "Am I doing enough?" "What if I miss an appointment?"

### History
- **Triggered by**: Loved one's diagnosis. Feeling overwhelmed by the coordination required. A bad experience at an appointment.
- **What they tried**: Spreadsheets. Group texts. Patient portals (no caregiver access). Calling insurance repeatedly.
- **What worked**: Having a single place for everything. Being able to share updates easily.
- **What failed**: Having to repeat information to every new doctor. Searching medical terms they don't understand.

### Key Objections
1. "The patient should handle their own care" → "Most patients want help — you're not overstepping"
2. "I don't have access to their records" → "They can invite you to their care circle"
3. "I'm not medical, I won't understand" → "We translate everything into plain language"

### AHA Moment
"Here are the 3 tests mom needs — share this with her doctor before Tuesday's appointment."

### Design Implications
- Care Circle tab is primary navigation for caregivers.
- One-tap sharing: send checklist, results, or questions to patient/family.
- Multi-patient support: can manage care for multiple people.
- Plain language by default, medical terms expandable.
- "Share with doctor" as a prominent action on every result.

## Using These Profiles

Before building ANY screen:

1. **Which profile(s) see this screen?**
2. **What emotional state are they in?**
3. **What's their #1 objection right now?**
4. **What's the ONE thing they need to do next?**
5. **What would make them feel [the emotion they WANT to feel]?**

If you can't answer these, you're not ready to design.
