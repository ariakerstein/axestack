# How to Create a New Category: The All-Encompassing Guide

## Introduction

Creating an entirely new market category – like HubSpot did with **Inbound Marketing** or Conversion Rate Experts with **Conversion Optimization (CRO)** – is one of the most ambitious strategies in business. It means introducing a novel concept or approach to solve a problem, giving it a name, and rallying an industry around it. This guide provides a comprehensive, step-by-step playbook on category creation, from foundational principles to real-world examples. It is designed for a reader with zero background knowledge – by the end, you’ll understand what it takes to design, launch, and **own** a new category, and be equipped to implement these ideas to get tangible results.

We will cover **why** creating a category can be so powerful (and risky), **how** to actually do it (step-by-step, with proven tactics), and lessons from diverse examples spanning B2B and B2C. Throughout, we’ll highlight what worked, what didn’t, common pitfalls, and how to think from first principles – including understanding customer Jobs to Be Done (functional, emotional, social jobs). By the end, you should be able to **make an informed decision** about pursuing category creation and have an actionable game plan to become a category expert and driver.

## Why Create a New Category?

Creating a new category is often described as the **“holy grail”** of marketing and strategy. When it works, the rewards are enormous. By defining a new space, you can position your company as the **undisputed leader** – the _category king_ – capturing outsized market share, growth, and customer mindshare. A Harvard Business Review study found that although only 13% of Fortune 100 companies were what we’d call “category creators,” those firms accounted for **53% of incremental revenue growth and 74% of incremental market cap growth** among the 100[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=of%20their%20business%20%20north,of%20%2438%20billion)[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=put%20the%20Customer%20Success%20category,negotiable%2C%20the%20category%20has%20exploded). In other words, companies that successfully establish new categories tend to massively outpace others in value creation.

_“Why it pays to be a category creator:” A study found that 13% of Fortune 100 companies (category creators) produced over half of the total revenue growth (53%) and nearly three-quarters of market cap growth (74%), far outpacing the contribution of the remaining companies[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=of%20their%20business%20%20north,of%20%2438%20billion)._

There are plenty of success stories that illustrate these benefits:

- **HubSpot and Inbound Marketing:** In 2006, HubSpot’s founders coined the term _inbound marketing_ to describe a new, pull-based marketing approach (attracting customers via content and search rather than pushing ads and cold calls). 17 years later, inbound marketing became a _household term_ in the industry, and HubSpot grew from a simple blog to a **$20+ billion company**, synonymous with its category[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=unique%20insight%20would%20upend%20traditional,hinge%20on%20defying%20conventional%20wisdom). HubSpot’s early decision to define and champion inbound marketing “became a $20+ billion success story” and the cornerstone of its brand[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=unique%20insight%20would%20upend%20traditional,hinge%20on%20defying%20conventional%20wisdom). By owning the inbound marketing category, HubSpot fostered a thriving community and achieved tremendous growth.
- **Conversion Optimization (CRO):** In 2007, the founders of Conversion Rate Experts (CRE) coined the term _“Conversion Rate Optimization”_ for the practice of scientifically improving website conversions[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=Conversion%20Rate%20Experts%20,buyers%20and%20clicks%20into%20customers). At the time, few businesses treated conversion improvement as a structured discipline. By naming and popularizing CRO, CRE helped create an industry – now, **over half of businesses** plan to increase CRO budgets year over year as conversion optimization has become a mainstream practice[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=According%20to%20a%202017%20study,their%20overall%20digital%20marketing%20strategy)[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=Conversion%20Rate%20Experts%20,buyers%20and%20clicks%20into%20customers). The term “CRO” is ubiquitous in digital marketing today, with entire communities, conferences, and roles (e.g. _CRO specialists_) dedicated to it. CRE’s own success (clients like Google, Facebook, Amazon, etc.[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=With%20clients%20in%20over%2040,that%20transcend%20borders%20and%20industries)[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=)) demonstrates how defining a category can position a company at the center of a high-growth trend.
- **Airbnb and the Home-Sharing Category:** Airbnb’s founders didn’t set out explicitly to “create a category,” but by solving an unmet need they _did_ create a new space – the **peer-to-peer short-term rental market**. When they rented out airbeds in their apartment in 2007, they tapped into a huge latent demand for alternatives to hotels. Airbnb ended up pioneering the _on-demand home-sharing_ category, which grew the company’s valuation to **$38 billion+** and spawned an entire ecosystem of hosts and guests[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=Their%20names%20were%20Brian%20Chesky,north%20of%20%2438%20billion). This illustrates that a powerful new category can emerge when an innovative solution meets a large unserved customer job (in this case, travelers seeking affordable, local accommodations).
- **Gainsight and Customer Success:** In the early 2010s, SaaS businesses were struggling with customer churn and needed a new approach to **retain and grow customers**. Gainsight seized this opportunity by championing “Customer Success” as a formal practice and software category. In 2013, when their team began evangelizing _Customer Success_, it was barely even a job title at most companies[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=Anthony%20Kennada%2C%20the%20Chief%20Marketing,negotiable%2C%20the%20category%20has%20exploded). Through relentless evangelism (conferences, content, a book by their CMO, etc.), Gainsight helped turn Customer Success into a thriving discipline. Over just a few years, the number of Customer Success Manager roles in the industry increased **400%**, and Gainsight’s revenue grew over **1000%** as they rode the category’s rise[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=Over%20the%20past%20four%20years%2C,but%20the%20payoff%20is%20real). By 2019, Gainsight was recognized alongside giants like Salesforce and HubSpot as a leading “category king” in tech[saastr.com](https://www.saastr.com/5-things-you-need-to-create-category-in-saas-gainsight/#:~:text=Anthony%20Kennada%3A%20Yeah,Salesforce%20and%20Marketo%20and%20HubSpot). This case highlights how defining a new category can not only drive one company’s growth, but also create an entire profession and community (benefiting customers and employees alike).

The _promise_ of category creation, then, is **market differentiation and dominance**. As marketing experts Al Ries and Jack Trout famously advised: “Forget the brand. **Think categories.** Prospects are on the defensive when it comes to brands… Everyone is interested in **what’s new**. Few people are interested in what’s _better_”[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=,22%20Immutable%20Laws%20of%20Marketing). By introducing something genuinely new (a new way to solve a problem), you can capture customers’ imagination far more than by offering just a slightly better product in an existing category. When you successfully create and own a category, you’re not just selling a product – you’re selling a vision of the future and _writing the rules_ of a game that you lead.

**However,** these big payoffs come with equally big challenges and risks. Before diving into _how_ to create a category, it’s crucial to understand what you’re signing up for and whether it’s the right move for your situation.

## The Challenges (and Risks) of Category Creation

Category creation is **not for everyone** – in fact, it’s a high-risk, long-term play that can strain resources and requires steadfast conviction. HubSpot’s co-founder Dharmesh Shah reflected on their journey: _“We did create the ‘inbound marketing’ category – and though that helped us with growth in the early years, it was hard, expensive, and risky. I don’t know that I’d recommend it for everyone.”_[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=,Dharmesh%20Shah%2C%20HubSpot). This sentiment is echoed by many who have walked the category path. Here are some key challenges and “gotchas” to be aware of:

- **It’s a Long-Term Marathon:** Launching a new category is not like a single product launch or campaign – it’s a sustained _multi-year_ (often decade-long) effort[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=You%20must%20have%20a%20clear,This%20is%20a%20marathon)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=,Dharmesh%20Shah%2C%20HubSpot). You must consistently _beat the drum_ for your idea over time. For example, HubSpot spent over a **decade** making “Inbound Marketing” a core concept in marketing[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=No%20matter%20how%20large%20your,of%20every%20brand%E2%80%99s%20marketing%20strategy). There are no overnight category successes; you need patience and long-term investment. If your company or team is only oriented to short-term results, category creation will be frustrating. **Lesson:** Ensure you have buy-in for a long journey and set expectations that this is a strategic marathon, not a sprint.
- **Significant Investment and Commitment:** Creating a category often requires substantial **content creation, education, community-building, and sometimes evangelizing in person** (events, talks) – all of which require budget and manpower. You’re essentially doing _market education_, which can be as intensive as marketing and selling a product (if not more). The payoff, if it comes, is down the road. In the meantime, it can be costly. **You need a clear vision and the stomach to invest heavily** in promoting an idea that initially has no existing demand[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=When%20HubSpot%20created%20%C2%A0%E2%80%9Cinbound%20marketing%E2%80%9D%2C,had%20more%20volume%20and%20awareness). (In the early days, _nobody_ was searching for “inbound marketing” – HubSpot had to create the awareness from scratch[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=When%20HubSpot%20created%20%C2%A0%E2%80%9Cinbound%20marketing%E2%80%9D%2C,had%20more%20volume%20and%20awareness).) Companies that succeed often dedicate teams to content and advocacy for years. If resources are tight or leadership isn’t fully committed, the effort may fizzle out before bearing fruit.
- **Educating the Market (Low Initial Demand):** By definition, a new category has no _existing_ market demand or search volume at the start[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=When%20HubSpot%20created%20%C2%A0%E2%80%9Cinbound%20marketing%E2%80%9D%2C,had%20more%20volume%20and%20awareness). Customers don’t yet know they have this problem or that a new solution category exists. This means early on you’ll face an awareness challenge: you have to simultaneously reveal a _problem_ people didn’t fully realize they had and convince them your new approach is the answer. Early traction can be slow. There’s a catch-22: you need people talking about and adopting the category for it to catch on, but at the start nobody is asking for it. **Conviction** is required to push through this phase[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=3)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=When%20your%20category%20is%20new%2C,drum%20and%20never%20gave%20up). Many companies give up too soon and revert to selling something more familiar – missing the chance to truly change the game. You have to have what Brian Balfour (HubSpot’s former VP of Growth) calls “blind faith” in your category vision during the early days[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=When%20your%20category%20is%20new%2C,drum%20and%20never%20gave%20up) – backed, of course, by clear evidence that you’re solving a genuine pain (more on that in a moment).
- **Risk of Failure (or Being Too Early):** For every Airbnb or HubSpot, there are examples like **Segway** or **Google Glass** – innovative technologies that _failed_ to find sustainable categories. Segway’s futuristic scooter was hyped as game-changing, but it never gained mass adoption, possibly because it didn’t solve a pressing _job to be done_ for enough people (it was a solution looking for a problem). Google Glass introduced “smart glasses,” but consumer concerns (privacy, utility, style) kept it from creating a viable new category at the time[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=Image%3A%20Creating%20a%20new%20product,category%20comparison%20of%20market%20entry). These cases show that even cool ideas can flop if the market isn’t ready or the value proposition isn’t clear. In fact, nearly **47% of first-movers fail** rather than becoming category kings[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=Image%3A%20Creating%20a%20new%20product,category%20comparison%20of%20market%20entry). Timing and genuine need are crucial – being first doesn’t guarantee success.
- **Requires Organizational Alignment:** Category creation isn’t just a marketing campaign; it often requires company-wide alignment around the vision. Your **product** may need to deliver a broad solution (not just a single feature) that embodies the category ethos. Your **team** needs to believe in and practice what you preach. For instance, HubSpot didn’t just talk about inbound marketing – they _ran their entire marketing_ as an inbound machine (blogging daily, renaming their own marketers as “Inbound Marketing Consultants,” etc.)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=aren%E2%80%99t%20doing%20it%20yourself)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=In%20HubSpot%E2%80%99s%20case%2C%20the%20company,%E2%80%8D). They even changed internal job titles – all marketers at HubSpot became “Inbound Marketers” to demonstrate commitment[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=aren%E2%80%99t%20doing%20it%20yourself). This level of internal buy-in makes the category narrative authentic. But it means your _sales_, _customer success_, and even _executives_ must rally behind the story, not just the marketing team. If there’s internal skepticism or mixed messaging, customers will notice.
- **Inviting Competition (Success Attracts Others):** Ironically, if you _do_ succeed in making a new category attractive, you won’t be alone for long. Competitors – both startups and incumbents – will notice the buzz and jump in, calling themselves “[Your Term] providers.” This is actually a sign you’re succeeding (the category is bigger than you), but it also means you must continue to _lead_ and differentiate. You essentially go from evangelizing the problem to also having to show why your solution is the best in the space. For example, once “customer success software” became a known category, big companies like Salesforce started using the term and offering related features, so Gainsight had to keep innovating and reinforcing its thought leadership to maintain its edge. **Gotcha:** Don’t assume creating the category alone guarantees permanent dominance – you must continuously invest in product, brand, and community to stay on top when others join the fray.

Given these challenges, **when is category creation the right strategy?** It usually makes sense if you have identified a **fundamentally new or vastly under-served problem** that current solutions do not address – _and_ your solution has the potential to become the go-to remedy for that problem. You should have a visionary outlook (where is the world heading, and what will customers need in a few years?) and the patience/resources to educate the market. Category creation is most effective when you’re not just trying to grab share in an existing market, but rather to **redefine the terms of competition** to your advantage. As we’ll discuss, a hallmark of category design is that you’re selling a _new problem_ (or a new way of looking at a problem) as much as a product. If your offering is truly novel and solves a big pain point or aspiration, and you’re willing to commit to _years_ of evangelism, category creation can be a game-changing strategy. Otherwise, you might consider more traditional differentiation or a “disrupt an existing category” approach instead of creating a whole new one.

If after this reality check you (and your stakeholders) are still excited about forging a new category, then let’s move on to **how** to do it effectively. The remainder of this guide breaks down the process into steps, complete with real examples and tactics.

## Step 1: Identify an Unmet Need or “Gap” (Jobs-to-Be-Done & Pain Points)

Every successful new category starts with a **first principle insight**: a recognition that there is an emerging or under-served **customer job to be done** or pain point that current categories don’t solve well. This step is about pinpointing _why_ the world needs a new category in the first place. Ask: _What change in the world or customer behavior has created a new problem or opportunity?_ What are users trying to achieve (functionally, emotionally, socially) that isn’t adequately addressed by existing solutions?

Begin by analyzing your target customers’ **Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)** in the area you’re exploring:

- _Functional jobs:_ What practical task or outcome do they need? (e.g. “Generate more high-quality leads for my business” or “Improve my website’s conversion rate.”)
- _Emotional jobs:_ How do they want to feel? (e.g. confident, not overwhelmed; proud of their work; less fearful of failure.)
- _Social jobs:_ How do they want to be perceived by others? (e.g. innovative among peers, providing great experiences to customers, etc.)

A new category often arises when a **functional job is not being met by existing approaches**, causing frustration (a pain point), and when solving it in a new way can also satisfy emotional or social aspirations. Let’s illustrate with examples:

- **Inbound Marketing’s Origin (HubSpot):** The _functional job_ for marketers was “attract customers cost-effectively in a digital world.” Circa 2005, traditional outbound tactics (cold calls, mass emails, print ads) were increasingly expensive and ineffective – customers were tuning out interruptive marketing[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=Imagine%20sitting%20down%20to%20dinner,the%20person%20selling%20the%20product)[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=creating%20a%20new%20way%20of,for%20marketing%20and%20customer%20relations). The _pain point:_ small businesses especially felt that “old game” marketing was broken (low ROI, annoyed prospects). HubSpot observed that with the rise of Google search, blogging, and social media, **buyer behavior changed** – people could find information on their own and were sick of being spammed[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=When%20HubSpot%20co,businesses%20over%20large%20enterprises%20to)[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=Imagine%20sitting%20down%20to%20dinner,the%20person%20selling%20the%20product). The _emotional job_ for marketers was to feel proud of how they market (not feeling like spammers) and more confident that their efforts aren’t annoying customers. The _social job_ was being seen as a modern, helpful marketer rather than a pushy salesperson. Recognizing this shift, HubSpot identified a gap: businesses needed a way to _get found_ by customers online through content and search, rather than chasing them. This was a problem that existing marketing software or tactics didn’t solve – it wasn’t about sending more emails or buying more ads, it was a fundamentally different approach. **Thus the idea of a new category was born**: an approach (and software) to _pull customers in_ – later named “Inbound Marketing.” The insight was rooted in a clear pain (outbound wasn’t working) and a new JTBD (attract interested buyers via valuable content).
- **Conversion Optimization’s Origin:** In the mid-2000s, many companies were pouring money into driving website traffic (SEO, ads) but neglecting the _functional job_ of turning that traffic into revenue. The pain point for online businesses: lots of visitors, few conversions, and no systematic method to improve. Marketers felt _emotionally_ that they were guessing about website changes, and _socially_ they lacked something impressive to show in growth metrics. Conversion Rate Experts (CRE) spotted this gap: companies needed a **scientific, repeatable process** to improve conversion rates. Traditional web design or marketing didn’t treat this as a distinct discipline – there was web analytics and there was marketing, but no “conversion optimization” field. CRE’s founders realized that by applying rigorous A/B testing and analysis, one could consistently lift conversions – a huge financial impact. The _job to be done_ was “get more customers or revenue from existing traffic.” They quantified that even small conversion lifts could mean big dollars, addressing a pressing need for businesses. Thus, the _Conversion Optimization_ category emerged to fill that need, offering both a methodology and consulting tools to achieve it. By coining the term and framing it as a must-do practice, CRE tapped into an unmet functional job (improve ROI without just buying more traffic) while giving marketers a new _social identity_ (“I’m a CRO expert”) that was professionally appealing.
- **Customer Success’s Origin:** Gainsight’s team, around 2013, noticed a unique pain in the SaaS industry: companies were losing too many customers after the sale (churn) because nobody was proactively ensuring those customers succeeded. The _functional job:_ “increase customer retention and lifetime value.” Historically, companies relied on support teams (reactive) or account managers for renewals, but there was no dedicated process/technology for _proactive customer success management_. As SaaS subscriptions grew, this became a critical gap – high churn could kill a business. The _emotional job_ for SaaS leaders was finding peace of mind that customers are happy and not constantly fearing surprise cancellations; for customer-facing teams, it was the satisfaction of helping customers achieve value (rather than only dealing with problems). The _social job:_ companies wanted to be seen as customer-centric and proactive. This led to the insight: **Customer Success** could be a new discipline and software category focused on nurturing customers post-sale. It addressed a pain that old categories (CRM, helpdesk, etc.) didn’t fully solve. Thus, Gainsight and others could create a category around managing customer outcomes, which resonated strongly with SaaS CEOs (because it hit the bottom line) and with a new breed of professionals (CSMs who found a career identity).

In each of these cases, **people had tried something before** – outbound marketing blasts, endless ad spend, reactive support – but those approaches were either failing or becoming less effective. The _new category_ offered a more efficient and effective way, often enabled by a technological or societal shift:

- Inbound marketing leveraged the internet and consumer info-search behaviors.
- CRO leveraged the availability of analytics tools and A/B testing frameworks.
- Customer Success leveraged the shift to subscription business models requiring ongoing customer engagement.

**Actionable Tip:** Start by **researching and listening**. Talk to potential customers and users: what are their biggest pain points in the area you’re innovating? What workarounds or hacks are they doing? A pattern of unmet needs can signal an opportunity. Look at trends – technology changes, regulatory changes, consumer habit shifts – that make an old approach outdated. Category ideas often come from observing “the way people are doing X is no longer optimal given the new reality.” As category design advisor Andy Raskin puts it: _“You’re not saying people were always wrong to play the old game. They’re only wrong now because **something in the world has changed** – there’s new knowledge, new technology, or new things buyers want.”_[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=problem%20with%20their%20existing%20behaviors). Identifying that **inflection point** – the change that makes a new way necessary – is the spark of category creation.

Finally, articulate the **aspiration**: What _better future_ does this category promise? For instance, inbound marketing promised a future where marketers **attract** customers by being helpful (a positive vision) instead of pestering them. That’s inspirational on top of being practical. If your prospective category doesn’t lend itself to an inspiring improvement in customers’ lives or businesses, it may struggle to gain emotional momentum. The best category visions resonate both logically (_“this will improve your metrics”_) and emotionally (_“this feels like a movement I want to be part of”_).

By the end of Step 1, you should have:

- A clear definition of the **problem/gap** and why existing solutions are insufficient.
- A sense of the **jobs-to-be-done** your category will fulfill (functional results, emotional benefits, social identity).
- Evidence (qualitative or quantitative) that this need is significant – e.g. data showing frustration with status quo, or success of early adopters of your new approach.
- A conviction that a _new concept_ (beyond just your product) is needed to truly address this need.

This forms the _foundation_ for all subsequent steps. You’ll use these insights to craft your category’s name, narrative, and strategy.

## Step 2: Name and Frame Your Category

**“The most underrated piece of marketing advice is that you’ve got to **name** something,”** says Dave Gerhardt, who helped Drift coin “Conversational Marketing”[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20most%20underrated%20piece%20of,%E2%80%9D). Naming your category is a pivotal act – it makes the abstract idea concrete and memorable. A good name captures the essence of your approach and gives people a way to talk about it. In many cases, the name itself can become a rallying cry or a job title (e.g. “I am an Inbound Marketer”).

**Characteristics of a good category name:**

- **Descriptive and Intuitive:** It should hint at the core idea without requiring a ton of explanation. _Inbound Marketing_ immediately suggests “marketing that draws people inbound.” _Conversational Marketing_ suggests “marketing through conversations.” _Account-Based Marketing_ clearly indicates “marketing based on specific accounts.” These names work because in a couple of words, the target audience gets the gist.
- **A Methodology or Approach (Not Just a Product):** HubSpot’s team emphasizes that _inbound marketing_ succeeded as a category name because it described a **methodology**, not just a branded term[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Step%20I%20%E2%80%94%20Naming%20and,Framing). It wasn’t “HubSpot Marketing” or some made-up word; it was an idea bigger than the company. This allowed many people (even outside HubSpot’s customers) to adopt the term and philosophy, which actually strengthened HubSpot’s position as the leader. Ensure your name represents a set of behaviors or a philosophy that others can embrace[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Step%20I%20%E2%80%94%20Naming%20and,Framing).
- **Positive or Aspirational Connotation:** Ideally, the name should have a positive ring. “Inbound” sounds welcoming (versus “outbound” which sounds pushy). “Customer Success” is inherently positive (who wouldn’t want success?). “Conversion Optimization” sounds like improvement and efficiency. Avoid names that carry negative baggage or are too tied to a problem; you want it to describe the _solution approach_. For example, “NoSQL” (a tech category) defined itself by what it’s not, which worked to an extent, but many modern category designers prefer framing in positive terms (“Document Database” or such might be clearer). HubSpot deliberately chose _Inbound_ (positive) rather than labeling their approach “Non-Outbound Marketing.”
- **Unique but Not Alien:** The name should be unique enough that you can own it (and ideally dominate SEO for it), but not so obscure that people can’t remember or pronounce it. In Gainsight’s case, the term _Customer Success_ was not widely used in software before, but it was plain English and intuitive – success for customers, who could argue with that? Conversion Rate _Optimization_ took an existing concept (conversion rate) and added a familiar word to imply a new practice. If you invent a completely new word, be cautious – you’ll have to educate people from scratch (which can work if done right, but is harder). Sometimes combining or re-framing existing words is very effective.

**Framing** goes hand-in-hand with naming. Framing means defining _what the category means and where it fits_. As you introduce the name, you must give it context:

- Provide a **clear definition**: e.g. “Inbound Marketing is a marketing approach focused on creating valuable content and experiences to attract customers, rather than broadcasting messages to a broad audience.” A one-sentence definition helps people grasp it quickly.
- Articulate the **problem it addresses**: e.g. “Why did we need _Conversational Marketing_? Because traditional lead forms and impersonal marketing weren’t engaging modern buyers – we needed a way to have real-time, personalized conversations at scale.” By framing your category as the answer to a previously unresolved problem, you give it importance.
- If possible, frame it as part of a broader **movement or trend**: e.g. “Customer Success is the answer to the subscription economy’s challenge – in a world where retaining customers is as important as acquiring them, we need new practices and tools.” This connects your category to something bigger happening in the world (which you likely identified in Step 1).

One of the best framing tools is to position your category **against an existing, well-understood idea** (the “old way”). This overlaps with the next step (creating an enemy), but it’s worth noting here: oftentimes the very _name_ can implicitly contrast with the old paradigm. _Inbound vs. Outbound Marketing_ is a perfect example: the name inbound inherently contrasts itself with outbound. The audience immediately asks “what is inbound? how is it different from outbound?” – and that opens the door for you to explain the advantages of the new category. In Drift’s case, they initially were described as “chat for sales” or “marketing chat” – but those phrases didn’t stick. It wasn’t until they _gave it a distinct name_ – _Conversational Marketing_ – that the concept took off[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=They%20Gave%20it%20a%20Name)[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CFrom%20there%2C%20people%20started%20to,%E2%80%9D). The name allowed them to escape the generic “chat tool” bucket and reframe how people thought about live chat, as a marketing must-have rather than just a support tool.

**Practical tips for developing a category name:**

1. **Brainstorm around the core idea and outcome.** List keywords related to the problem and solution. Think about the _noun_ that describes the domain and an _action_ or approach that describes the method. (Inbound Marketing = inbound + marketing approach; Account-Based Marketing = marketing focused on accounts; etc.) If your category is a technology platform, sometimes “Platform” or “Automation” or “AI” etc. get added, but be cautious – a mouthful name like “Intelligent Predictive Analytics Platform” is not catchy. Shorter is better.
2. **Listen to your users’ language.** Dave Gerhardt of Drift kept a file of how others described their product[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=We%20often%20talk%20about%20the,conceptualize%20and%20describe%20your%20company)[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=Dave%20keeps%20a%20file%20of,%E2%80%9D). Often, your early adopters might naturally coin phrases – pay attention to those. You want a name that resonates with how customers _already think_ if possible. For example, perhaps some early user said “This feels like conversational marketing, not just chat.” Such clues are gold.
3. **Practice and socialize internally.** Try using the name in sentences, in marketing copy, and see if it flows. Get your team’s feedback – does it feel right when they say “We are a [Category Name] company” or “We provide [Category Name] solutions”? Early discomfort might mean it’s not intuitive enough.
4. **Check SEO and uniqueness.** You want to ideally own the Google results for the term. If the phrase is too common, it might be hard to dominate (e.g. “Modern Marketing” would be too generic). If it’s unique but unpronounceable, that’s another issue. Strike a balance.
5. **Be patient if needed.** It might take time and several iterations to land the perfect name. It’s _so_ important that Drift’s team said “be patient and the words will come” – they played with the concept and let it mature until “conversational marketing” clicked[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=,and%20the%20Words%20Will%20Come)[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20most%20underrated%20piece%20of,%E2%80%9D). Don’t rush to coin jargon for its own sake; make sure it truly fits the idea.

When you have a promising name, **declare it** confidently. Use it in your marketing materials, your website headlines, your LinkedIn bios. You want to start owning that term. For instance, Drift changed their messaging site-wide to herald “the leader in Conversational Marketing,” and even ensured their metadata and Google snippet reinforced that identity[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=It%20wasn%E2%80%99t%20just%20Drift%E2%80%99s%20product,industry%3B%20it%20was%20their%20philosophy). HubSpot’s founders literally titled their early blog “on start-ups, inbound marketing and more” to plant that flag, and they eventually wrote the book _“Inbound Marketing”_. Naming is an act of creation – once you name it, treat it as _real_ and others will start to do the same.

Before moving on, double-check: Does your category name clearly convey the key idea? Does it differentiate from what came before? Will your target audience be _proud_ to use that term to describe themselves or their strategy? If yes, you have a strong starting point to frame the larger story around.

## Step 3: Create a Compelling Enemy (Old Way vs. New Way)

Every good story needs conflict – and in category design, that means defining an **enemy** (the status quo or old way) that your new category defeats. This isn’t about attacking a person or a specific competitor; it’s about personifying the _old problem_ or approach as something outdated and inferior (the villain), to make your _new approach_ (the hero) more compelling. By vividly contrasting **“Before vs. After”** or **“Us vs. Them”**, you help customers understand _why_ they should care about changing to your category.

HubSpot mastered this with _Inbound vs. Outbound_. The enemy was _outbound marketing_ – essentially the traditional tactics of cold-calling, spamming, print ads, etc. Interestingly, HubSpot actually **coined the term “Outbound Marketing”** as well[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=For%20HubSpot%2C%20it%E2%80%99s%20%E2%80%98Outbound%20Marketing%E2%80%99%2C,with%20the%20term%20%E2%80%98outbound%20marketing%E2%80%99)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=the%20term%20%E2%80%98outbound%20marketing%E2%80%99)! They put a label on the old way, which made it easier to target as a foe. By saying “outbound marketing is broken, and inbound marketing is the answer,” they created a clear divide. Marketers could instantly relate: _“Oh yes, outbound is the stuff we hate doing (and customers hate receiving). Inbound sounds like the opposite – something better.”_ This enemy framing was crucial: **If HubSpot had simply said “Here’s a new marketing software called HubSpot that does things differently,” it wouldn’t have resonated as deeply.** Instead, they essentially said: _“Outbound marketing (the way you’ve been doing things) is increasingly ineffective in today’s world – consumers have changed. The enemy is the old playbook. We propose a new playbook: inbound marketing.”_

Why is having an enemy so important?

- It creates a sense of _urgency_ and _purpose_. If there’s no enemy, there’s no rush to change. By pointing out the flaws or pain caused by the old way, you tap into dissatisfaction. For example, “outbound marketing is interruptive and inefficient; marketers are wasting money and annoying customers.” This makes the reader think, _yes, I do feel that pain._ It primes them to seek an alternative.
- It gives your community something to unite **against**. People adopting a new category often feel they’re part of a movement to overturn the old order. It’s almost tribal – “we’re the inbound marketers, fighting against the old interruptive tactics.” This bonding over a common adversary (even if it’s an abstract one) can strengthen the category’s community.
- It clarifies the **choice**. Great category creators often present a stark choice: continue with the status quo (which is now reframed as the _losing side of history_) _or_ embrace the new way (the future). Andy Raskin’s storytelling framework (often used in pitches) emphasizes highlighting a **shift** in the world that makes the old way untenable[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=problem%20with%20their%20existing%20behaviors). You’re not necessarily saying people were stupid before, but that _now_ there’s a better way because conditions changed[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=problem%20with%20their%20existing%20behaviors). This allows customers to save face – they weren’t wrong to do outbound or ignore conversion before, but now that there’s a new option and new environment, sticking with the old ways is “wrong.”

**How to define your enemy:** The enemy is usually the **existing behavior or solution that your category aims to replace or surpass**. It could be:

- An old _technology_ or software category (e.g. for Salesforce’s SaaS CRM, the enemy was on-premise CRM software and the idea of installing software – hence their famous “No Software” logo, making traditional software the enemy).
- An old _approach or philosophy_ (e.g. Gainsight positioned the old mindset of treating customer management as an afterthought as the enemy – instead of proactive Customer Success, the enemy was reactive support or simply hoping your product speaks for itself).
- A _problem_ that’s been long ignored or accepted as “just the way it is.” For example, the category of DevOps (as a movement) set up “siloed dev and IT teams with slow, waterfall deployments” as the enemy, promoting a new collaborative culture as the hero.
- Sometimes, the enemy can even be an emerging threat in customers’ world. For cybersecurity categories, for instance, the enemy might be “the new sophisticated cyber attacks that old tools can’t handle,” implying we need a new category of defense.

A quick checklist from Brian Balfour’s insights on picking the right enemy[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=A%20quick%20checklist%20to%20help,pick%20the%20right%20enemy):

1. **Base it on existing behaviors:** What are users doing today that is painful or suboptimal? (E.g., marketers were blasting emails and cold calls – painful and low yield.)
2. **Make it relatable:** The audience should instantly recognize the enemy as something they struggle with. HubSpot knew marketers _already_ disliked doing spammy outbound as much as customers hated receiving it[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=1,this%20was%20the%20only%20way). That relatability made the enemy credible.

**Gotcha to avoid:** Don’t frame the enemy as _the customer themselves_. You’re not telling them _“you have been dumb/wrong”_ – you’re saying _“the old methods/tools haven’t kept up – not your fault, but now there’s a better way.”_ Notice HubSpot’s approach: they didn’t shame marketers; they empathized that marketers hated spam too but felt it was the only way. Then they offered a new option. This approach invites people to willingly switch sides, rather than putting them on the defensive.

Let’s look at a couple more examples of enemy framing:

- **Drift (Conversational Marketing)**: The enemy they often cited was the **lead capture form** and the impersonal marketing funnel. Drift’s narrative was that forcing every website visitor to fill a form and wait for a sales reply was a bad experience – in today’s real-time world, it’s akin to ignoring someone who walks into your store. They highlighted how buyers were frustrated with slow, one-way communication (that’s the pain of the old way), and that businesses were losing leads due to this. So the old practice of static forms and marketing automation drip emails became the “enemy” (they even had bold blog posts like “**Forms are Dead**”). Conversational Marketing, with live chat and AI bots engaging instantly, was the hero solution. This framing made it clear: if you’re still just using forms, you’re behind – join the new way of chatting in real time.
- **Account-Based Marketing (Terminus/ABM)**: The enemy here was _spray-and-pray lead generation._ ABM proponents (like Terminus’s Sangram Vajre) pointed out the wasted effort in broad marketing where ninety-something percent of leads go nowhere. The old funnel, casting a wide net, was inefficient for B2B. ABM framed that traditional demand gen as the wrong approach, and made “flipping the funnel” (targeting specific high-value accounts first) the new winning strategy[dtso.org](https://www.dtso.org/startups/category#:~:text=By%20inverting%20the%20B2B%20sales,%E2%80%A2%20Canva). By demonizing the generic funnel (“leads for leads’ sake”) and championing alignment with sales on target accounts, ABM advocates gave B2B marketers an enemy (random acts of marketing) and a hero identity (focused marketer who drives real revenue).

Once you have your enemy defined, incorporate it everywhere in your narrative:

- On your website and whitepapers: explicitly compare the old vs new. Charts, tables or graphics can help (HubSpot famously had “Inbound vs Outbound” visuals to show the differences[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=,)). A simple contrasting table of “Old Way vs New Way” is a powerful clarity tool.
- In your pitches and content: tell the story of why the old way fails _now_. For example: _“In 2005, if you cold-called a prospect, they might listen. Today, they don’t – they screen calls and ad-block everything. Outbound marketing worked in the past, but in the 2020s it’s a losing battle – buyers have all the power. That’s why inbound marketing is crucial.”_
- Use metaphors or anecdotes: E.g., Gainsight would talk about how customer success was like “treating customers like a garden that needs tending” whereas the old way was letting weeds grow until it’s too late – giving a vivid image of the old neglect vs new care.

**Emotional angle:** Often the enemy can also represent an emotional frustration. In the HubSpot case, outbound marketing was not just inefficient; it made marketers _feel bad_ (who likes annoying people?). In the Customer Success case, the enemy was the feeling of being in the dark about your customer health and scrambling when things go wrong – a source of stress for account managers and CEOs alike. By defeating that, the new category promises peace of mind and pride in being proactive. So, highlight not only the rational flaws of the old way, but also the emotional relief of moving to the new way.

In summary, clearly **name your villain**. Make it _the problem_, not the customer. Make it specific and tied to behaviors the customer will recognize in themselves. Once they agree that the enemy is real and unsustainable, they’ll be primed to hear how your category (and your product) can help them triumph over it.

## Step 4: Craft the Category Narrative (Hero Story & Manifesto)

With the stage set – you’ve identified the need and named your approach and the enemy – now you must tell a **compelling story** around your category. Think of this as writing the **manifesto** or the _“Declaration of Independence”_ for your movement. It should answer: _Why does this category exist? What change does it propose? How will it make the customer (the hero) win the day?_

Key elements of a category narrative:

- **Hero:** The hero of the story is **your customer/user**, _not_ your company. Frame the customer (or the role) as the protagonist who will undergo a transformation. For example, in HubSpot’s narrative, the hero was the _Marketer_ who discovers inbound marketing and becomes vastly more effective (and happier) than the old “interruption marketer.” HubSpot positioned itself as a mentor/tool-giver to the marketer hero. Similarly, Gainsight’s narrative made heroes of _Customer Success Managers_ and _forward-thinking CEOs_ who champion customer success – they are the ones who drive higher growth by embracing this philosophy.
- **Villain:** As discussed, the villain (enemy) must appear in the story as the obstacle to overcome. Outbound Marketing was cast as the villain causing pain to both marketers and customers. Whatever enemy you identified in Step 3, weave it into the narrative as the antagonist force that the hero battles.
- **Resolution through the New Way:** Show how the hero defeats the villain by adopting the new category approach. This is where you illustrate the _benefits and outcomes_ of the new way. In storytelling terms, “enter [Category Name].” For instance: _“Trapped in the cycle of outbound marketing, the marketer was struggling – until inbound marketing came along. By creating content that customers love, the marketer started attracting leads effortlessly, while competitors still pounding the phones fell behind.”_ This paints a clear before-and-after picture.

**Storytelling techniques that work:**

- **The “Old World vs New World” framing:** Describe the _world before_ your category, and the _world after_. Many category manifestos start by painting a picture of how things used to be (usually bleak or fraught with a certain problem), then highlight a _shift or discovery_, and then describe the new world where the problem is solved or greatly mitigated by the new category. For example, an excerpt from a hypothetical manifesto: _“For decades, software companies focused only on acquiring customers – once the deal was closed, customers were left on their own (old world). But the rise of SaaS changed the game: if customers don’t get value continuously, they leave (shift). That’s why a new era of **Customer Success** is dawning – one where businesses win by ensuring their customers win (new world). In this new world, companies who embrace Customer Success grow faster, and Customer Success Managers are the linchpin of revenue growth.”_ This type of narrative helps the reader _see themselves_ crossing the chasm from old to new world.
- **Manifesto page or document:** HubSpot famously created an _“Inbound Marketing Manifesto”_ page[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Step%203%20%E2%80%94%20Storytelling) and a dedicated “story” section on their site explaining _why_ inbound was needed and what it entailed. This served as a go-to reference for anyone curious about the concept[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Step%203%20%E2%80%94%20Storytelling). Consider creating a whitepaper, ebook, or detailed blog post that lays out the philosophy of your category. This isn’t a sales pitch for your product, but rather an educational and inspirational pitch for the idea itself. It should be something you’d be happy to have even non-customers read and nod along with.
- **Use of Visuals and Metaphors:** Visual storytelling can dramatically reinforce your narrative. HubSpot’s success was partially attributed to powerful visuals contrasting inbound vs outbound[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=When%20it%20comes%20to%20a,dichotomy%20that%E2%80%99s%20hard%20to%20replicate). They depicted things like magnet vs megaphone, or created infographics showing how a stranger becomes a customer through inbound methods. Visual dichotomies (like a side-by-side comparison of the hero vs villain approaches) stick in people’s minds. If you can reduce your story to an image or a catchy metaphor, it spreads faster. For example, the _“Flip My Funnel”_ graphic (by ABM proponents) was a visual way to show the difference between the traditional funnel and the ABM inverted funnel – one picture that conveys the new philosophy.
- **Make it Personal:** Great narratives often include the _human element_. Perhaps share a specific story of a person or company who exemplified the change. For instance, Airbnb’s narrative could include the story of that first host who paid rent by sharing their space, creating a personal connection to the broader idea of home-sharing. For a B2B category, maybe highlight a forward-thinking professional who tried the new way and became a hero in their company. This gives readers a character to identify with.

**Example – HubSpot’s Storytelling:** HubSpot’s early storytelling (as recounted in various interviews) went something like: _“The way people shop and learn has changed. The old playbook of cold calls and ads is yielding diminishing returns (setup). We were two grad students who noticed our own habits – we were tuning out ads and consuming search results and blogs (inciting insight). We realized businesses needed to match how modern consumers behave. So we started experimenting, and inbound marketing was born – a collection of blogging, SEO, social media strategies to **attract** customers by being helpful (the new solution). We wrote the book on it. Now thousands of companies are seeing success by switching to inbound (proof/validation). Join us, and become an inbound marketer – your customers will thank you” (call to action/invitation)._ They essentially turned marketing from a company-centric activity into a _cause_ that marketers could champion on behalf of customers. This narrative is empowering: it casts the marketer as a hero who can rise above annoying tactics and actually _delight_ customers, while outperforming competitors. It’s both practical and moral – almost giving a sense of doing the _right thing_ (helping not harassing) while winning business.

**Make the Customer the Hero of the story.** This point cannot be overstated. Your narrative should invite your audience to see _themselves_ as heroic participants in this new movement. HubSpot didn’t say “HubSpot will defeat outbound marketing for you.” They said, implicitly, “_You_, the marketer, can defeat outbound by adopting inbound (with HubSpot’s help).” Gainsight talked about CEOs and CSMs who _transformed their companies_ by focusing on customer success, with Gainsight as the platform enabling it – but the glory goes to the practitioners in the story, not just the vendor.

**Building Identity:** If you do it right, people will start identifying with your category’s moniker. Marketers began to call themselves _“Inbound Marketers”_ proudly – it even became a job title people put on resumes and LinkedIn[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=The%20reason%20why%20%E2%80%98Inbound%20Marketing%E2%80%99,could%20also%20identify%20with%20it)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Because%20it%20was%20easy%20to,This%20made%20it%20sticky). That’s a huge win – it means your category has taken on a life of its own. Likewise, we saw the rise of “Customer Success Manager” as a common title in SaaS, and “Conversion Optimization Specialist” in e-commerce teams. When your narrative provides not just a solution but an _identity_ (“I am part of this movement/profession”), adoption can skyrocket because it taps into social and emotional drives. People love to be part of something that feels bigger than themselves.

To foster this, consider giving your audience a label they’ll embrace. HubSpot used “inbound marketers” and even named their annual conference **INBOUND**, reinforcing that identity. If your category is “DevOps”, you call practitioners “DevOps engineers” which became a standard term. Some categories even create certifications or badges (HubSpot’s Inbound Marketing Certification, for instance) so that people can _prove_ they belong to the club, which further cements the community and spreads the word.

**Write down your manifesto and key messages** clearly, and ensure everyone on your team knows the story. Consistency is key – the narrative should be the backbone of all your communications. It might feel repetitive to you, but remember most people will encounter it in bits and pieces, so hammering the same story home across channels is actually beneficial.

By the end of this step, you should have:

- A written narrative (manifesto, about page, pitch deck section, etc.) that outlines the problem (villain), the change, and the solution (hero’s journey with the category).
- Visuals or analogies that make the story memorable.
- A simple way to explain it in person: a 30-second elevator pitch version (“In the old world… but now, because [change], we need [Category]. It lets you do X, Y, Z, unlike the old way. It’s basically [short analogy]. Example: [very brief success story].”).
- Ideally, a slogan or rallying sentence. For example, Gainsight often said “**Customer Success is about driving outcomes for your customers, and that drives your growth.**” A crisp line like that helps anchor the narrative.

With a strong narrative in place, you’re ready to _broadcast_ it far and wide. That’s where evangelism and drum-beating come in.

## Step 5: Evangelize and Educate the Market (“Beating the Drum”)

Now it’s time to take your category story to the world. Think of this as **building the movement**. The goal is to _consistently expose_ your target audience to the new category idea and win converts – turning skeptics or the unaware into believers and practitioners. This requires a sustained marketing and evangelism effort across multiple fronts. Brian Balfour notes that **figuring out ways to _consistently_ get your market to adopt the new behaviors is where most companies fail at category creation**[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Figuring%20out%20ways%20to%20consistently,is%20where%20most%20companies%20fail). So this step is critical: you must “beat the drum” for your category, loudly and relentlessly, until it breaks through.

Here are the key tactics and channels to do so, along with examples of what has worked:

- **Content Marketing Machine:** Almost every successful category creator heavily invested in content. **Educational, valuable content** that ties back to the category narrative is perhaps your greatest weapon. HubSpot’s blog became one of the most popular marketing blogs in the world, publishing new posts almost _daily_, each teaching some aspect of inbound marketing or related skills[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=%E2%80%8DContent%3A). Importantly, _every_ piece of content either directly or subtly reinforced the inbound philosophy[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=%E2%80%8DContent%3A). This created an enormous tailwind: people searching for marketing tips would find HubSpot’s content, learn about inbound, and become intrigued by the approach. Over years, this content attracted millions to the idea. Similarly, Conversion Rate Experts published in-depth case studies and guides on CRO – teaching others how to do A/B testing, sharing results they got for clients, etc. By freely sharing knowledge, they not only proved the value of the approach but also positioned themselves as the authority. **Takeaway:** Start a dedicated blog, video channel, or publication for your category. Populate it with how-tos, research, case studies, thought leadership – anything that helps your audience do the job better using the new philosophy. Optimize this content for search so that when people start looking up the category term or related problems, they find you. Content also gives you a steady stream of material to share on social media and newsletters, keeping the drum beating continually.
- **Publish Definitive Resources (Books, Guides, Reports):** A signature move in category creation is to author the “Bible” of the category. HubSpot’s founders literally wrote the book _Inbound Marketing_ (published 2009)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=directly%20or%20indirectly%20tied%20the,readers%20back%20to%20%E2%80%98Inbound%20Marketing%E2%80%99), which became a bestseller and introduced the concept to a wider audience. Conversion Rate Experts co-authored a book on Conversion Optimization. Gainsight’s CMO Anthony Kennada wrote _Category Creation_ (2019) sharing the playbook and evangelizing Customer Success[categorydesignadvisors.com](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/category-creation-dominates/#:~:text=In%20the%20book%2C%20Category%20Creation%2C,emerging%20business%20strategy%20into%20perspective)[categorydesignadvisors.com](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/category-creation-dominates/#:~:text=In%20Part%20I%20of%20his,a%20category%20in%20Part%20II). Drift’s CEO David Cancel co-wrote _Conversational Marketing_. These books and guides serve a dual purpose: they codify your ideas (giving credibility and clarity) and act as high-value marketing assets (speaking at events or media is easier when you’ve literally written the book on it). Even if not a full book, consider creating _ultimate guides_ or annual research reports that people in your industry will cite. For example, since CRO was new, ConversionXL (another company in that space) started an annual “State of Conversion Optimization” report that gave stats and benchmarks – useful info that also underscored that CRO is an established, growing practice. These authoritative resources help _legitimize the category_ in the eyes of skeptics.
- **Live Events and Conferences:** Bringing people together under the banner of your category is hugely powerful. HubSpot launched the **INBOUND conference** (starting in 2010 with just a few hundred people, growing to tens of thousands in later years). This wasn’t a user conference about HubSpot software – it was a conference about the _inbound marketing movement_. By hosting it, HubSpot became virtually synonymous with the category, but they featured many speakers, even those not associated with HubSpot, as long as they aligned with the inbound philosophy. Gainsight did something similar with their **Pulse conference** for Customer Success, which grew into one of the largest SaaS conferences with thousands of attendees annually, all coming to learn and celebrate customer success practices. Running a major conference is a big lift, but even on a smaller scale, hosting meetups or webinars under a category-themed series can foster community. The key is to make the event about learning and networking around the idea (not just a sales pitch). If your category really resonates, people will be excited to attend and meet peers. It cements a sense of _community (more on that in the next step)_. An important side-effect: events create _FOMO_. When others see their peers attending a “Conversational Marketing Summit” or an “ABM Workshop,” they get curious – “is this something I should be doing?” That’s how you gradually convert the mainstream.
- **Influencer and Thought Leader Involvement:** Partner with respected voices who align with your vision. HubSpot smartly involved well-known marketing gurus like **Seth Godin** to speak about inbound marketing, thereby borrowing credibility[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Influencer%20Marketing%3A%E2%80%8D). They also invited influencers to contribute content or join webinars. This third-party validation is crucial – if only you talk about your category, it can seem like a marketing ploy; but if independent experts also champion it, the idea gains legitimacy. Identify thought leaders (authors, bloggers, industry analysts, prominent practitioners) who believe in the problem you’re solving. Share your POV with them, offer data or case studies, and encourage them to discuss it. Maybe co-host a podcast or co-author an article. The more voices spreading the message, the faster it propagates. One caution: ensure authenticity. HubSpot succeeded partly because the influencers they tapped (like Godin) genuinely cared about improving marketing and saw inbound as positive – it wasn’t just paid endorsement, it was aligned philosophy[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=favor). Keep it real: people can smell when a “thought leader” is just regurgitating a vendor pitch versus when they truly find the concept valuable.
- **Early Adopter Success Stories:** Turn your early customers or adopters into **advocates**. When someone uses your approach and wins, amplify that story. HubSpot encouraged successful customers to blog or speak about how inbound marketing transformed their business[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Leveraging%20Early%20Adopters%3A%E2%80%8D). Those peer examples carry weight – it’s not just the inventor saying it works, it’s a _user_ saying “this changed my results.” Create case studies that focus on the category angle (e.g. “Company X switched from outbound to inbound marketing and saw 3X more leads” or “Y Company implemented an Account-Based Marketing strategy and doubled their enterprise pipeline”). Encourage these customers to present at conferences or in webinars. Essentially, **enable word-of-mouth** among your target community. Eventually, you want the conversation to shift from _you_ pushing the idea to the community talking to each other about it. As Balfour notes, category creation depends more on _what your audience says to each other_ than what you say[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=HubSpot%20identified%20and%20converted%20early,%E2%80%8D). So stoke those conversations. A user writing “Why I became an [Category] convert” on Medium, or answering questions on forums recommending the approach, is gold.
- **Community Content & Social Proof:** Create spaces for the community to share and connect. This could be an online forum, LinkedIn group, or hashtag where practitioners discuss tips. HubSpot launched _Inbound.org_ (an online community for marketers to share articles and discuss inbound topics). Such platforms not only spread knowledge but signal that “many people are doing this.” Social proof helps those on the fence feel safer to try the new thing because others are clearly on board. Also, highlight metrics of adoption if you have them: e.g. “Over 100,000 marketers have taken our Inbound Marketing certification” or “5,000 people attended the Pulse conference this year – up from 300 just 5 years ago, reflecting the explosive growth of Customer Success.” Concrete numbers or milestones give the sense that the train is leaving the station – better hop on.
- **SEO/SEM and Own the Term:** Ensure that when people search your category name or problem, they find you. HubSpot did a great job of dominating SEO for “inbound marketing” for obvious reasons. They also smartly ran Google Ads on terms like “outbound marketing” to educate searchers about inbound as an alternative. In Gainsight’s case, early on few searched “customer success software,” so they focused on content around “customer churn” and “account management best practices” – then introduced the new term within that content. As interest grows, you might even use Google Trends or search volume as a KPI to see how awareness of the category is spreading.
- **Public Relations (PR) and Analyst Relations:** Use PR to tell the category story in the media. Rather than press releases about product features, pitch stories to industry publications about “the rise of [Category].” For instance, an article like “Why Inbound Marketing is the Next Big Thing for SMBs” featuring HubSpot’s viewpoint, or a trade magazine piece on how CRO is changing e-commerce. If you can afford a PR agency or have contacts, get journalists to cover the challenges of the old way and mention your category as the new solution. Similarly, engage industry analysts (Gartner, Forrester, etc.). While analysts are often slow to accept new terminology (they prefer to categorize everything themselves), showing them client demand and traction can eventually lead to them naming the category in reports or creating a Magic Quadrant. Being recognized by an analyst report as “leading vendor in [Category]” pretty much cements the category as official. Gainsight and others worked hard to convince analysts to write about Customer Success Management as a software segment – it took time, but now it’s a known category partly due to those efforts.

All these tactics boil down to **repetition and consistency**. “Beating the drum” means you keep broadcasting the message through every available channel, such that your target audience encounters it _again and again_. The first time, they might shrug it off. The third or fourth time, perhaps they start to investigate. By the tenth time, they might be adopting it. In HubSpot’s case, a marketer might read a blog post, then hear about inbound at a conference, then see someone on LinkedIn share an infographic, then finally decide “Okay, I keep hearing about this inbound marketing – let’s check it out.” That’s when they perhaps download an ebook or attend a webinar, and the conversion to believer begins.

One more point: **Live it yourself**. A credible evangelist _practices what they preach_. HubSpot famously _was_ its own best case study: they generated the bulk of their early customers via inbound marketing (blog + SEO + free tools), not via big ad spends. They could say “we got to 15,000 customers with zero cold calls” as a proof point. They also internally changed job titles to inbound as mentioned and focused their entire marketing budget on inbound tactics. This authenticity is persuasive. Likewise, if you’re preaching conversational engagement, ensure your own site uses chat and personal touches (Drift did this – no forms on their site at all). If you advocate for data-driven experimentation (CRO), run many experiments on your own site and publish the results. By _living the category_, you not only get better at it, but you demonstrate confidence in it.

To summarize this step: Deploy a **multi-channel campaign of ideas**. Content, events, influencers, customers, PR – orchestrate them to constantly reinforce the category narrative. This phase often lasts _years_. HubSpot hammered “inbound” from 2006 onward without let-up; even a decade later they still framed new products in context of inbound marketing and later the “inbound methodology” expanded to sales and service. You have to be prepared to champion the cause well beyond the initial launch buzz. As Dharmesh Shah put it, a big launch event is not enough – you have to continue beating the drum **“for years”**[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=2). If you do, the noise builds into a real movement.

Next, we’ll discuss cultivating the community and ecosystem around your category – which is closely intertwined with evangelism, but deserves its own focus.

## Step 6: Build a Community and Ecosystem Around the Category

A new category will flourish when it becomes **bigger than just your company** – when it evolves into a community, or even an industry, that others participate in. Your role shifts from sole creator to **steward and leader** of a movement. This is a critical mindset: you want to enable anyone who aligns with the category to join in, contribute, and benefit. If the category stays a one-man show, it won’t reach its full potential. Here’s how to cultivate a thriving ecosystem:

- **Foster a Community of Practitioners:** As interest grows, formalize avenues for practitioners to connect with each other. This can be through user groups, online forums, Slack/Discord communities, social media groups, etc. For example, marketers embracing inbound congregated on Inbound.org (which HubSpot supported but was a community-driven content forum). Gainsight started a community where Customer Success pros share strategies. If your category is technical, platforms like Stack Overflow or GitHub might be relevant for community Q&A or open-source tools. The idea is to let people who are excited about the category help each other, swap tips, and feel a sense of belonging. **Your company can host or moderate, but let the users’ voices shine.** Over time, super-users or advocates will emerge; celebrate them (maybe an ambassador program or MVP awards). Remember, **“categories are built by customers,” not just companies**[categorydesignadvisors.com](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/category-creation-dominates/#:~:text=a%20company%20that%20has%20a,categories%20are%20built%20by%20customers)[categorydesignadvisors.com](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/category-creation-dominates/#:~:text=With%20focused%20efforts%20on%20activating,likely%20to%20stand%20behind%20a) – so engage your early adopters deeply. Treat them as co-creators of the movement.
- **Conferences and Meetups (User-Led):** Beyond the large flagship events you host, encourage regional meetups or user-organized events. HubSpot’s INBOUND conference spawned local HubSpot User Groups (HUGs) where inbound marketers meet regularly. These might not focus on your product at all, but on the techniques of the category. Provide support: maybe give them some funding, swag, or content to use. If people are willing to gather on their own to talk about your category, that’s a huge sign of a solid community. Facilitate it.
- **Certification and Training Programs:** Create a pathway for people to become “certified” experts in the category. HubSpot Academy’s Inbound Marketing Certification (free online course) trained hundreds of thousands in inbound principles – many of whom were not HubSpot customers at first, but they learned the methodology and earned a certificate. This does several things: it **standardizes knowledge**, ensuring quality practice; it creates a talent pool (employers start seeking “inbound certified” marketers, further entrenching the approach); and it turns learning the category into an accessible, shareable achievement (people post their certificates on LinkedIn, etc., giving the category more exposure). Gainsight likewise developed customer success certification programs. If your category is technical, consider certification for developers or consultants in the new skill. By formalizing education, you signal that this is a _real discipline_ with best practices and recognized expertise levels.
- **Partner and Competitor Ecosystem:** This might sound counterintuitive, but for a category to really establish itself, you often need multiple players, even including competitors, to be part of the ecosystem. If you’re the only one talking about it, outsiders might dismiss it as “just your marketing shtick.” But if several companies – including competitors or tangential products – also use the term and align their offerings, it solidifies the category’s presence. For example, Terminus (an ABM platform) actively partnered with other companies (some competitive, some complementary) to create the _#FlipMyFunnel_ community and spread ABM[frontlines.io](https://www.frontlines.io/podcasts/creating-the-account-based-marketing-category-with-sangram-vajre/#:~:text=,their%20own%20category%20creation%20journey). They realized that if multiple vendors and agencies all push ABM, it creates more customer demand than Terminus alone could. Gainsight’s CMO mentions partnering with competitors to grow the Customer Success category[frontlines.io](https://www.frontlines.io/podcasts/creating-the-account-based-marketing-category-with-sangram-vajre/#:~:text=,their%20own%20category%20creation%20journey) – essentially a rising tide lifts all boats. This requires confidence in your leadership position, but if you have that, don’t shy from welcoming others under the tent. It could mean co-sponsoring events, cross-posting content, or at least maintaining a friendly stance that “we may compete, but we all agree this new approach is the future.” When others start identifying with _your term_ and building services around it, you know the category has legs independent of you.
- **Customer Ambassadors:** Leverage your happiest customers as **category ambassadors**. Invite them to speak at events or in webinars not just about your product, but about their success with the approach generally. Users often have more credibility with peers. Also consider case competitions or awards – e.g. an annual “Inbound Marketing Awards” for the best campaigns, or “Customer Success Innovator of the Year.” HubSpot did something like this with their annual awards at INBOUND, showcasing spectacular inbound campaigns by various companies (some not even customers). This celebrates the community’s achievements and motivates others to strive for similar recognition, reinforcing best practices. As Kennada highlights in his book, activating customers into **category ambassadors** can drive growth – their authentic advocacy and peer influence propel the category forward[categorydesignadvisors.com](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/category-creation-dominates/#:~:text=a%20company%20that%20has%20a,categories%20are%20built%20by%20customers)[categorydesignadvisors.com](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/category-creation-dominates/#:~:text=With%20focused%20efforts%20on%20activating,likely%20to%20stand%20behind%20a).
- **Culture and Values in the Community:** A community isn’t just tactics and tips – it’s often bonded by shared values or ethos. Kennada notes that building a human-first, values-driven community was key to Gainsight’s category success[categorydesignadvisors.com](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/category-creation-dominates/#:~:text=It%20doesn%E2%80%99t%20come%20as%20a,of%20the%20driving%20forces%20behind). They emphasized that Customer Success is about empathy and win-win outcomes, which attracted people who resonated with that mindset. HubSpot’s community valued being helpful and transparent (they gave away so much content free, reflecting a generosity value). Consider explicitly stating the core values of your category movement. For instance, if you’re creating a category around ethical AI, one of your values might be transparency. These values will attract like-minded customers and employees, and help maintain a positive culture in your events/forums. It also makes the category feel like a force for good, which increases emotional attachment.
- **Monitoring and Nurturing:** Keep a pulse on the community’s health. Is engagement growing? Are there common questions or misconceptions arising? Use this feedback to adapt your content and narrative. Sometimes a community will even take the category in new directions – listen and adapt if the usage or scope of the category broadens. E.g., “inbound marketing” started primarily about content, SEO, and social, but later included things like conversion offers, even sales alignment (Inbound Sales). HubSpot embraced that evolution, expanding their academy and content to cover the full _“Inbound methodology”_ from marketing to sales to service[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=HubSpot%20evolved%20a%20lot%20and,the%20effects%20of%20%E2%80%9CThe%20Flywheel%E2%80%9D). Staying involved ensures the category stays relevant and you remain seen as a leader in it, not a dictator but a guide who evolves the story with input from the crowd.

In essence, building community is about **letting go of some control** and enabling others to take partial ownership of the category. It can be humbling (others might critique or modify the concept), but it’s necessary for scale. The outcome you want is a self-sustaining ecosystem: customers identifying strongly as category members, experts emerging, other companies aligning to it, and a general consensus that “this is a _thing_ now.”

When that happens, you’ve truly succeeded – your brand is forever linked to the creation of something new and valuable, and even if competitors abound, you often enjoy a halo as the pioneer. As one advisor put it, you need to create a movement _“that anyone could stand behind and feel connected to”_, not just something your company owns[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Community%3A). HubSpot reached a point where “Inbound Marketing” was a term _everyone_ used (even their competitors and detractors)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Community%3A). It became a movement marketers could rally around, which in turn drove more business to HubSpot because they were the instigators.

By cultivating community, you effectively turn your customers into your best marketers. Their passion and word-of-mouth will drive adoption far beyond what your own campaigns could achieve alone.

## Step 7: Sustain, Evolve, and Measure the Category’s Success

Creating a category is not a one-and-done effort – even after you achieve initial traction, it’s an ongoing process to maintain category leadership and adapt to changes. In this final step, we address how to **keep the momentum** going in the long run and ensure your category remains relevant (especially as we head into 2025 and beyond, with rapidly changing trends).

- **Continuous Innovation and Relevance:** As time passes, make sure your category _keeps up with the times_. This might mean expanding the definition or scope as new technology or practices emerge. For instance, HubSpot didn’t stop with inbound marketing for just attracting leads. They later extended the inbound philosophy to sales (_Inbound Sales_) and customer service, and introduced the idea of the **“Flywheel”** model to augment the traditional funnel[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=HubSpot%20evolved%20a%20lot%20and,the%20effects%20of%20%E2%80%9CThe%20Flywheel%E2%80%9D). They recognized that just generating leads wasn’t enough; to truly embrace inbound in 2020s, you must delight customers so they become promoters (hence the flywheel of attract, engage, delight). By evolving their narrative, HubSpot kept inbound marketing fresh and aligned with current challenges. Similarly, if you pioneered something like “DevOps”, later you might incorporate “DevSecOps” as security becomes vital, etc. **Watch for signs** that your category narrative needs an update – perhaps new objections arise, or the market’s focus shifts slightly. As the leader, you should be first to address these and weave them into your story, so the category stays the solution to the most pressing problems.
- **Maintain Thought Leadership:** Don’t rest on your laurels. Continue researching, learning, and sharing insights related to your category. As the space matures, people will look for advanced knowledge. Provide that – maybe via updated books, level-2 certifications, more nuanced frameworks. Also, as competitors join, you want to stay the intellectual leader. Keep publishing data or vision pieces. For example, Gainsight after a few years started talking about “Customer Success 2.0” – a reimagining of what CS would look like in a product-led era, etc. By doing so, they reinforced that they’re still pushing the frontier of the category. Use your vantage point (lots of customers, years of experience) to produce unique insights that newcomers can’t. This ensures loyal followers continue to look to you for guidance.
- **Category Performance Metrics:** Just as you track product metrics, track the growth of your category. Some possible KPIs:

  - **Awareness metrics:** Search volume for category keywords, social media mentions, media references. Google Trends is a handy tool – e.g., watching “inbound marketing” go from zero to mainstream. Also, analyst coverage (did Gartner create a category or start mentioning it?). If the term makes it into industry surveys or list of job titles on LinkedIn, that’s a measurable sign.
  - **Community metrics:** Number of conference attendees, forum members, certification graduates, meetup groups. For instance, if your community site membership keeps rising, or your annual conference grows 50% YoY, those are concrete indicators of momentum.
  - **Customer adoption metrics:** If possible, quantify how many companies or users have adopted the approach (beyond just your customers). For example, “X% of marketing teams now practice inbound marketing” or “67% of B2B organizations have some form of account-based marketing in 2024”[demandgenreport.com](https://www.demandgenreport.com/resources/as-abm-demand-gen-converge-orgs-focus-on-data-enrichment-retention-plays/48567/#:~:text=,plan%20to%20add). Industry surveys can provide these stats – often you can sponsor one or encourage a research firm to study the trend. By publicizing that “the majority are now doing X,” you both show success and create peer pressure on the laggards.
  - **Business impact metrics:** Ultimately, does owning the category translate to results for you? Monitor your market share in that category (though in a new category, you might define the metrics yourself). Many VCs and analysts will look at, for example, what portion of marketing software spend goes to “inbound” tools or how large the CRO services market has become. When HubSpot went public, part of their pitch was the size of the inbound marketing market they helped create. Harvard’s study we cited earlier linked category creation to outsized revenue and valuation growth[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=of%20their%20business%20%20north,of%20%2438%20billion), which can be an argument to your investors or board that the strategy is paying off.
  - **Qualitative signals:** Are customers and press using your terminology unprompted? When competitors start describing themselves with _your_ category term, oddly, that’s a success indicator (as long as you keep differentiation). It means the category is bigger than you. Also, listen to sales calls or customer conversations – do prospects come saying “We need to do [Category] and are looking for solutions”? When that starts happening regularly, you’ve created demand beyond brand-driven awareness – they’re not just looking for your brand, they’re looking for the category, which increases the whole pie.

- **Defend against Category Drift:** There can be challenges as the category matures. One is **category drift**, where the term gets overused or misused to the point of confusion. For example, as inbound marketing got famous, some agencies slapped “inbound” on services that were not truly inbound-y, or SEO firms rebranded as inbound marketers without embracing the whole methodology. This could dilute meaning. As a category leader, address this by continually educating on what _good practice_ looks like, perhaps establishing standards or guidelines. Another challenge is competitors trying to redefine the category to their advantage. If that happens, double down on your POV and, if needed, refine your messaging to clarify distinctions. Sometimes a category might even split into sub-categories as it grows (like “marketing automation” later split into B2B vs B2C tools or into “customer engagement platforms,” etc.). Stay on top of these shifts – you might choose to broaden your umbrella or to focus on a specific subcategory where you remain king.
- **Know When to Expand vs. When to Focus:** In sustaining the category, there’s a tension: you want the category to be large and inclusive, but you also don’t want it so broad that it loses coherence. Over time, you might be tempted to stretch the category meaning to cover more use cases (especially as your product line grows). Do so carefully. If you stretch too far, people might get confused about what the category really means. A good approach is to keep the core definition consistent, but show how it connects to adjacent areas. HubSpot didn’t rename inbound marketing when applying it to sales; they kept the inbound concept but talked about “inbound sales” as a natural extension, preserving consistency. So maintain a clear core even as you explore new angles.
- **Adapt to the 2025 Landscape:** Given it’s now 2025, consider new tools and platforms to aid category growth:

  - **Leverage AI and personalization** to spread your message – for instance, AI-driven content recommendations in your community, or personalized learning paths in your Academy. Also, if AI is impacting your industry, weave that into your category narrative (maybe the category approach is even more important in the age of AI, etc.).
  - **Virtual events and webinars** have become mainstream (accelerated by the pandemic era), so continue with those as they can reach a global audience cheaply.
  - **Micro-communities** (like specialized subgroup forums or Telegram channels) might form – monitor them.
  - And importantly, **sustainability and social impact** are big considerations now – if relevant, highlight how your category contributes to a better world or aligns with larger societal trends (e.g., inbound marketing was also about respecting customers’ time and attention – in an era of privacy concerns and consumer empowerment, that spin could be emphasized even more).

- **Stay Humble and Customer-Centric:** A final cultural point for longevity: maintain humility and the _“learn-it-all”_ mindset rather than a “know-it-all” mindset[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=focus%20for%20the%20entire%20organization,of%20continuous%20learning%20and%20adaptation). Dharmesh Shah from HubSpot advocates this – just because you created the category doesn’t mean you have nothing left to learn[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=focus%20for%20the%20entire%20organization,of%20continuous%20learning%20and%20adaptation). Encourage feedback from new entrants, keep listening to customers’ evolving needs, and be willing to adapt. That authenticity and dedication to solving the customer’s evolving job will keep your category relevant and your company trusted to lead it.

By doing all of the above, you ensure that your category doesn’t fade as a short-lived buzzword, but rather becomes a permanent part of the business lexicon. The ultimate sign of success is when your category is considered _common sense_ – future professionals can’t imagine a time before it existed. Marketing students today learn about inbound marketing in textbooks; product teams think about customer success as a standard function; e-commerce teams routinely run CRO experiments – these concepts have become part of the norm. At that point, your category is truly established, and your early efforts have paid off in creating a lasting legacy (and likely a very robust business for your company as well).

## Diverse Examples of Category Creation (Successes and Lessons)

To make these principles more concrete, here is a roundup of diverse examples across industries – including both **success stories** and **cautionary tales** – and the takeaways from each. These illustrate how the category creation playbook can be applied in different contexts and what can be learned from each attempt:

- **Inbound Marketing (HubSpot) – _Success_:** We’ve discussed HubSpot at length because it’s a textbook case. Key factors in its success: a _memorable name_, clear enemy (outbound), massive content output, and community building (the INBOUND conference, HubSpot Academy). HubSpot also quantified the success of inbound marketing: e.g. by 2014, they celebrated acquiring their 3,500th customer largely through inbound methods[hubspot.com](https://www.hubspot.com/blog/bid/7033/3-500th-customer-chooses-hubspot-and-inbound-marketing#:~:text=3%2C500th%20Customer%20Chooses%20HubSpot%20and,challenges%20and%20inbound%20marketing), and today their brand is practically synonymous with the approach[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=unique%20insight%20would%20upend%20traditional,hinge%20on%20defying%20conventional%20wisdom). _Lesson:_ A well-framed methodology can become ubiquitous when supported by education and evangelism. **Quantifiable impact:** Inbound marketing today is part of nearly every marketer’s toolkit, and HubSpot’s market cap > $20B is partially attributed to the category they championed[sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/podcast/crucible-moments-hubspot/#:~:text=unique%20insight%20would%20upend%20traditional,hinge%20on%20defying%20conventional%20wisdom).
- **Conversion Rate Optimization (Conversion Rate Experts & Others) – _Success_:** In the mid-2000s, “conversion rate optimization (CRO)” wasn’t a common term. Conversion Rate Experts (CRE) coined it around 2007[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=Conversion%20Rate%20Experts%20,buyers%20and%20clicks%20into%20customers), and firms like Invesp and Optimizely also drove it. The name is a bit technical, but it clearly described the goal (optimize your conversion metric). The category caught on as companies saw the ROI of testing. By 2017, half of marketers said CRO was crucial[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=According%20to%20a%202017%20study,their%20overall%20digital%20marketing%20strategy), and by 2023 a majority planned to increase CRO budgets[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=Fast,metric%20in%20evaluating%20revenue%20performance) – evidence that this concept firmly took root. CRE’s insistence on evidence (A/B test everything) helped give CRO credibility[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=What%20sets%20CRE%20apart%20is,back%20guarantee%20underscores). _Lesson:_ Even a niche practice can grow into a significant category if you show measurable success and coin a term that organizations can rally around. Also, being first to articulate a best-practice (and publishing a lot about it) can establish you as the leader – CRE landed clients like Google and Facebook[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=With%20clients%20in%20over%2040,that%20transcend%20borders%20and%20industries)[figpii.com](https://www.figpii.com/blog/guides/cro/agencies/#:~:text=) by being the trusted experts in the category they popularized.
- **Account-Based Marketing (Terminus & others) – _Success_:** The term “Account-Based Marketing (ABM)” actually existed in some form earlier, but it wasn’t mainstream until around 2014-2015 when startups like Terminus, Engagio, and Demandbase heavily evangelized it. Sangram Vajre of Terminus co-founded the company and spearheaded a movement, even creating the #FlipMyFunnel conference and community[dtso.org](https://www.dtso.org/startups/category#:~:text=By%20inverting%20the%20B2B%20sales,%E2%80%A2%20Canva). They framed ABM’s enemy as the inefficient broad demand gen funnel. The timing was right: marketers were overwhelmed with low-quality leads and sales was frustrated – ABM promised better alignment and higher ROI by targeting best-fit accounts. The category exploded; by the late 2010s, surveys showed about **2/3 of B2B organizations had ABM programs** in place[demandgenreport.com](https://www.demandgenreport.com/resources/as-abm-demand-gen-converge-orgs-focus-on-data-enrichment-retention-plays/48567/#:~:text=As%20ABM%20%26Demand%20Gen%20Converge%2C,plan%20to%20add) and many were increasing spend[thecmo.com](https://thecmo.com/demand-generation/abm-statistics/#:~:text=CMO%20thecmo,ers%20Measure%20Return%20on%20Investment). Big players like Adobe and Oracle acquired ABM-focused companies, validating the space. _Lesson:_ Even if you don’t strictly “invent” a term, you can own it by being the loudest champion and giving it structure (like the FlipMyFunnel framework). Also, building a coalition – Terminus partnered with other vendors to create an ABM Alliance – can quickly grow a category’s presence. The ABM story also highlights the importance of timing (rise of personalization tech, sales-marketing alignment need) in helping a category win.
- **Customer Success (Gainsight) – _Success_:** Gainsight’s creation of the Customer Success category shows how to create not just a product category but a whole **profession**. They coined phrases like “Customer Success Manager” as a role, and pitched that CEOs need a Chief Customer Officer, etc. They poured effort into events (Pulse conferences globally), content (blogs, a book, even fun music videos to make it viral), and influencing Silicon Valley investors to value CS. The result: nearly every SaaS company today has a Customer Success team; the title “VP of Customer Success” is common, and there’s a flourishing ecosystem of CS tools and meetups. Gainsight’s revenue soared and they achieved a high valuation before being acquired – proof that leading the category paid off. _Lesson:_ Solve a pain that has clear business impact (reducing churn), name it simply, and build a passionate community around helping each other succeed. Gainsight also demonstrated the power of **values** in a category – by stressing a human, customer-centric culture, they made Customer Success more than a tactic; it became a mindset, which is very sticky. Quantitatively, the 400% increase in CSM jobs in a few years[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=Over%20the%20past%20four%20years%2C,but%20the%20payoff%20is%20real) is a metric showing how a category can create an entire job market.
- **Conversational Marketing (Drift) – _Success (with caveats_):** Drift coined “Conversational Marketing” in 2016-17 to reframe live chat and chatbots as a core marketing channel. They executed many category tactics right: a clear enemy (static lead forms), a book published on the concept, hiring evangelists, and offering free training. They even created a certification and a community around the idea of real-time engagement. Their growth did spike – Dave Gerhardt noted **Drift’s growth exploded after naming the category**[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=Image%3A%20conversational%20marketing%20drift%20article). They quickly rose in valuation and brand recognition. However, one could argue the category didn’t fully monopolize the narrative long-term as “conversational marketing” somewhat overlaps with broader “conversational commerce” or “chatbots” categories that others push. Still, Drift successfully differentiated against bigger chat software players for a time by owning this narrative. _Lesson:_ Even in a crowded market, strategic repositioning and naming can carve a unique category. But also note, if the concept is not radically distinct (chat for marketing vs chat for support – a subtler shift), it may be easier for others to adopt or for the term to dilute. One must keep pushing thought leadership or risk bigger players co-opting the space (e.g., many CRM companies added chat and could claim they too do conversational marketing). So far though, Drift remains strongly associated with the term and continues to release AI innovations under that banner[prnewswire.com](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/drift-defines-future-of-conversational-marketing-with-new-ai-powered-innovations-301945215.html#:~:text=Drift%20Defines%20Future%20of%20Conversational,one%20conversation%20at%20a%20time).
- **Salesforce and “No Software/Cloud CRM” – _Success_:** Salesforce in its early days (2000s) didn’t coin “cloud computing” but they popularized software-as-a-service for CRM. Their category was essentially **Cloud CRM**, setting them apart from traditional CRM software like Siebel. Marc Benioff famously used the “**No Software**” slogan – portraying installed software as the enemy (complete with the 🚫 software logo everywhere). He evangelized “the end of software” era and the move to web-based applications. This narrative was bold and risky in the early 2000s, but it worked fantastically, aligning with the emergence of the internet. Salesforce built a massive community (Dreamforce conference is one of the biggest tech events now) and essentially became the category king of CRM by defining the _new category of on-demand CRM_. _Lesson:_ You don’t always need a completely new name; sometimes framing an existing category with a new paradigm (on-demand vs on-premise) can be the category angle. Also, bold stances (“software is dead”) can garner attention – an example of using an extreme enemy to make your point. Salesforce’s $200+ billion market cap speaks to how valuable owning the “Cloud CRM” category was.
- **Blue Ocean Strategy examples – _Success_:** While not tech, the concept of _Blue Ocean Strategy_ (creating new market space) gave some classic examples: e.g., **Cirque du Soleil** created a new category of entertainment (circus + theater for adults, with premium ambiance) by moving away from traditional circus elements like animal acts and child focus. They tapped into an unmet audience – adults willing to pay for high-end artistic circus experiences – and built a multi-billion dollar business, basically making old circuses irrelevant. **Nike** with its Nike+ and later fitness tracking created a category of “connected fitness experiences” beyond just shoes. **Starbucks** transformed coffee from a commodity into a premium “café experience” category (the “third place” concept). Each of these identified a different value proposition that wasn’t being offered and turned it into a category of one which others then copied. _Lesson:_ The principles of category creation apply beyond tech – focus on noncustomers, create new value factors, and communicate a new experience. The result can be a leap in demand and a new normal in the industry (today, any new circus act is compared to Cirque, coffee shops emulate Starbucks’ model, etc.).
- **Failed or Challenging Attempts:** It’s instructive to consider attempts that didn’t fully pan out:

  - **Segway (Personal Transporter):** Hyped as creating a new category of personal urban transport in 2001. Despite massive buzz, it sold poorly. Why? The product didn’t truly fit into people’s daily jobs-to-be-done (walking short distances wasn’t a big enough problem, and there were barriers like high cost and regulatory issues on sidewalks). The category narrative (“this will change how cities are built!”) was grandiose but lacked practical foundation. _Lesson:_ No matter how innovative, a “category” will fail if it doesn’t solve a real, widespread need or if environmental factors (laws, social norms) block adoption. Sometimes timing is off or product execution doesn’t live up to the promise. Segway shows that tech alone doesn’t make a category – the use-case does.
  - **Google Glass (Wearable AR for consumers):** Touted a new category of consumer augmented reality eyewear in 2012. It did generate the concept of “wearables,” but Glass itself flopped commercially. The term “Glasshole” (for obnoxious users) indicated the social pushback. Privacy concerns and lack of clear killer apps meant consumers weren’t won over by the narrative of ubiquitous computing on your face. Google pivoted the product to enterprise use later. _Lesson:_ If a category challenges social norms (like wearing a camera on your face) without adequately addressing the fears and without strong consumer pull, it may backfire. New categories must account for emotional/social acceptance as much as pure function. In contrast, later wearables like Fitbit found more acceptance by focusing on fitness (a job people understood) with minimal social disruption.
  - **Webvan (Online Grocery Delivery):** In 1999, Webvan tried to pioneer online groceries, a category ahead of its time. The concept made sense (people want convenience), but execution was too costly/early (few people had broadband, unit economics were terrible). Webvan went bankrupt, but a decade later, Instacart, Amazon Fresh, etc. successfully built that category when enabling tech and consumer readiness improved. _Lesson:_ Timing is crucial. You might have the right idea but the wrong era. Being a category creator often means being early – but _too_ early can burn through resources before the market matures. An informed category designer today should scan for enabling factors: do enough potential customers have the pain and capability to adopt the new solution now, or are you betting on a future that’s still far out?
  - **Excessive Hype without Substance:** Occasionally, companies try to slap a new category label on an old idea without real differentiation, hoping marketing alone will make it stick. These usually fizzle. For example, if someone tried to rename “email marketing” as “Conversational Email Synergy” (hypothetically) without a truly new approach, it’d be seen as gimmick. Customers are savvy; they adopt new categories when there’s genuine value or a new way of thinking, not just rebranding. _Lesson:_ Ensure your category story has _depth_ – a unique methodology, a change in practice, or technology that delivers a leap in value. Otherwise, it may be dismissed as a buzzword.

- **Emerging Categories to Watch (2025 and beyond):** To adapt our thinking to the current landscape, consider areas now bubbling up:

  - **“Generative AI” as a category:** OpenAI and others have effectively created a new category of AI tools that can generate content. They gave it a name, and now “Generative AI” is a hot category with many entrants. The narrative is that traditional software couldn’t create, now AI can. The enemy might be blank screens or slow manual creation. This category is still shaping, but it shows how quickly a term can catch on when the tech reaches a tipping point.
  - **“Product-Led Growth (PLG)” as a go-to-market category:** Coined around mid-2010s and popularized by firms like OpenView, PLG defines a strategy where the product itself drives acquisition (free trials, freemium). It’s become a key concept in SaaS. There’s now a community of PLG enthusiasts, events, etc. It wasn’t one company’s product, but a collective category that many companies use in positioning. _Lesson:_ Sometimes categories can be about business model or strategy, not just a product category (in a way, similar to inbound marketing as a strategy). If you can name a successful new strategy, you can become a thought leader even if you’re not selling a product for it (OpenView is a VC, yet PLG is strongly associated with their content).
  - **“Metaverse” – category in progress:** Facebook (Meta) is trying to push the “metaverse” as a new category of social technology (VR/AR world). They renamed the company, investing billions. It’s a high-stakes category creation bet. So far, the concept has a lot of hype but also skepticism – its success is uncertain, as practical use and broad need are not clear yet. _Lesson:_ Even the biggest companies take swings at new categories; success will depend on whether they can identify or create compelling use cases for ordinary people. The key jobs (virtual socializing, work, play) need to be markedly better in the metaverse than current options to justify the shift.

Each example reinforces the core principles we’ve covered, while adding nuance:

- A strong **POV and clear problem/solution** drives success.
- Timing and context matter immensely.
- Execution (content, community, product delivering on the promise) is what turns an idea into a lasting category.
- Not every attempt succeeds – but even failures teach what to avoid (e.g., don’t ignore the end-user’s real needs, don’t overshoot current technology).

By studying these, an associate-level professional can see patterns to emulate and pitfalls to steer clear of when attempting category design in their own field. **Diverse examples make it easier to translate the playbook to new fields**, because you can analogize: “What’s the ‘inbound vs outbound’ dynamic in my industry? Who could be the Seth Godin influencer figure here? Are we facing a scenario like Webvan (too early) or like Salesforce (just right with a bold stance)?”

Now, with both the framework and examples in mind, we conclude with some final actionable tips and a checklist to begin your category creation journey.

## Conclusion & Action Plan

Creating a new category is undoubtedly a **challenging endeavor**, but as we’ve seen, it can be one of the most impactful moves for a company or even an entire industry. You’re essentially aiming to _change how people think and behave_, not just selling a product. This guide has armed you with a deep understanding of how to do that – from identifying the right problem, crafting a name and narrative, all the way through evangelizing, community-building, and sustaining the momentum.

To summarize, here are the **key takeaways** and a condensed **step-by-step action plan** for implementation:

1. **Find the Gap and Define the Why:** Start with customer pain points and jobs-to-be-done. What’s broken or incomplete about the status quo? Articulate the change in the world that makes the old approach ripe for disruption[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=problem%20with%20their%20existing%20behaviors). Ensure the problem is significant and widespread enough to merit a new category.
2. **Craft a Compelling POV (Name, Enemy, Narrative):** Coin a crisp, intuitive name for your category – one that represents a methodology or solution, not just your product[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Step%20I%20%E2%80%94%20Naming%20and,Framing). Identify the “enemy” (old way) that your category opposes, and make it relatable and urgent[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=For%20HubSpot%2C%20it%E2%80%99s%20%E2%80%98Outbound%20Marketing%E2%80%99%2C,with%20the%20term%20%E2%80%98outbound%20marketing%E2%80%99)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=For%20HubSpot%2C%20the%20enemy%20was,how%20%E2%80%98Inbound%20Marketing%E2%80%99%20was%20born). Develop the hero story with your customer as protagonist, overcoming that enemy through the new way. Write a manifesto or tagline that encapsulates the vision.
3. **Build Credibility with Evidence:** Before (and as) you evangelize, gather any evidence that your approach works. This could be internal data, pilot customer successes, or industry stats that reinforce your argument (e.g., “47% of first-movers fail, but category creators see outsized rewards”[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=As%20the%20likes%20of%20Airbnb%2C,of%20incremental%20market%20cap%20growth), or your own case studies). This helps convince the skeptics and analytically-minded folks that the category isn’t just hype but grounded in results.
4. **Evangelize Relentlessly Across Channels:** Launch a content blitz – blogs, webinars, whitepapers – educating the market on _how to do_ this new thing and _why it matters_. Use SEO to capture interest, and social media to amplify. Line up speaking engagements or host meetups. Essentially, wherever your audience spends time, inject the category narrative there consistently. Make sure to leverage influencers and early adopters to echo the message[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Influencer%20Marketing%3A%E2%80%8D)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=Leveraging%20Early%20Adopters%3A%E2%80%8D).
5. **Create a Movement, Not a Marketing Campaign:** Invite others to participate. Start a community forum or Slack. Encourage user groups or an annual summit. Offer free training or certification so people can proudly join the ranks of certified practitioners. Highlight user success stories and give them a platform. The goal is a self-sustaining ecosystem where many voices carry the torch, not just you.
6. **Maintain Focus and Patience:** Don’t get discouraged if traction is slow initially – category creation is a long game[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=1.%20Long)[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=No%20matter%20how%20large%20your,of%20every%20brand%E2%80%99s%20marketing%20strategy). Stick to your narrative; avoid diluting it for short-term gains (like using legacy terms for quick SEO wins at the expense of your vision – though you might tactically bridge old to new in content). Continuity and repetition are your allies. Also, be ready to invest significant resources (time, budget, creative energy) consistently. As Dharmesh Shah said, it was “hard, expensive, and risky”[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=,Dharmesh%20Shah%2C%20HubSpot) – know that going in, and secure support from leadership by sharing cases like HubSpot and Gainsight to show the payoff.
7. **Adapt and Iterate:** As your category starts to gain recognition, gather feedback. Which parts of your story resonate most? Are people misunderstanding any aspects? Use this to refine your messaging. Stay agile – if a particular term isn’t clicking, tweak it (Drift played with how to describe their offering until “conversational marketing” stuck[leadfeeder.com](https://www.leadfeeder.com/blog/conversational-marketing-drift/#:~:text=They%20Gave%20it%20a%20Name)). If you discover new benefits or use cases, incorporate them. Keep the core idea but evolve details in response to the market.
8. **Measure Impact and Celebrate Milestones:** Keep track of key metrics – e.g. rising search trend for your category, number of community members, etc. When you hit milestones (1000 customers doing things the new way, or the first time an analyst uses your term, or reaching 10,000 forum members), celebrate them publicly. This not only is good internal motivation, it also shows outsiders that the movement is growing (“momentum marketing”). It builds credibility that “hey, this is happening.” For instance, you might publish “State of [Category]” reports annually showing growth, much like how inbound marketing’s growth was tracked by HubSpot in annual benchmark reports.
9. **Secure the Base, then Expand:** Once your category is established in its initial domain, you can extend it thoughtfully. But ensure you’ve truly dominated the initial niche first. Being the category king in a defined space often yields ~70-80% of the market’s economics to you[intercom.com](https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/disrupt-or-create-a-category/#:~:text=As%20the%20likes%20of%20Airbnb%2C,of%20incremental%20market%20cap%20growth). Only after that should you leverage your position to broaden (like HubSpot did from marketing into sales CRM, etc., under the inbound umbrella). Expanding too early or into unrelated areas can confuse the category narrative.
10. **Stay Customer-Centric and Ethical:** Lastly, always tie back to making your customers’ lives better. A category will only thrive long-term if it genuinely delivers value and is trusted. Don’t oversell beyond what you can deliver – meet the heightened expectations you set. Engage customers in two-way dialogue; let them shape the future. Maintain integrity in messaging. This ensures you build not just a category, but goodwill and a positive reputation around it (which makes people _want_ to join your movement).

By following these steps, an associate-level professional can develop the expertise to spearhead category creation initiatives and drive real results. This guide has equipped you with a rich toolkit – from theoretical frameworks to concrete examples and data points – so you can move forward confidently. Whether you’re at a startup trying to break free of incumbents by defining a new space, or at an established company looking to reframe the conversation, the approach is similar: **identify a compelling truth that no one is talking about, give it a name, and lead the charge in solving it.**

In 2025’s fast-moving business environment, new categories are emerging all the time – and existing ones being redefined – often around innovations in AI, sustainability, remote work, and more. The opportunities to apply category design thinking are plentiful. Using first principles and the structured approach from this guide, you can adapt to any domain. Just remember, at the heart of category creation is understanding people’s aspirations and pains (functional, emotional, social) and offering a narrative that promises a better path[grain.com](https://grain.com/blog/category-creation-with-brian-balfour#:~:text=1,this%20was%20the%20only%20way)[categorydesignadvisors.com](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/category-creation-dominates/#:~:text=a%20company%20that%20has%20a,categories%20are%20built%20by%20customers). Focus on that, and you’ll not only make an informed decision about whether to pursue category creation, but if you do, you’ll have a roadmap to becoming an expert in it and achieving meaningful impact.

Good luck on your category creation journey – go create the next big thing that people didn’t even know they needed! 🚀
